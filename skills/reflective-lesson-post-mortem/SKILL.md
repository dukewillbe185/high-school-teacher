# Reflective Lesson Post-Mortem

**Description**: Systematically analyzes failed lesson segments to extract insights for future instructional design and professional development planning.

## When to Use

Use this workflow when a lesson segment has failed or underperformed, specifically when students can identify literary devices (e.g., "emotive language") but cannot explain their effect or purpose. It is designed for real-time diagnosis using student responses rather than relying solely on the original lesson plan.

## Process

1. **Diagnose in Real Time**: Check student responses immediately; do not rely on the original lesson plan if it predicts success without evidence. If a student identifies a feature but cannot explain the effect, treat this as a conceptual gap, not a lack of effort.
2. **Narrow the Scaffold**: Immediately pivot from abstract analysis to providing a clear bridge, such as a sentence frame, to reduce cognitive load. Model one example aloud and ask the student to try the same structure with a different sentence.
3. **Embed Structural Changes**: For future cohorts, add an earlier diagnostic task to test whether students can explain effect, not just identify techniques.
4. **Redesign the Sequence**: Extend the duration of modelling and guided practice before moving to independent work.
5. **Plan Proactively**: Do not wait for misunderstandings to appear mid-lesson; plan for them upfront by embedding success criteria and sentence stems around the link between choice, purpose, and audience impact.

## Decision Criteria

- **Misdiagnosis Risk**: Relying solely on the original lesson plan without checking student responses can lead to misdiagnosis.
- **Identification vs. Explanation**: Do not treat the student's identification of a feature as a complete success if they cannot explain the effect.
- **Frustration Management**: When a student becomes frustrated, keep the tone calm and frame the scaffold as temporary support, not a limitation (e.g., "You are not wrong—you've spotted...").
- **Cognitive Load**: If a standard prompt requires choosing evidence, structuring, and explaining effect all at once, the cognitive load is too high.

## Edge Cases & Failure Modes

- **Overestimation of Prior Knowledge**: Students may overestimate their knowledge, requiring the teacher to move slower than planned.
- **Low-Level Errors**: A common error is labeling a device (e.g., "emotive language") and stopping there without explaining why the writer chose it.
- **Scaffolding Limits**: Scaffolding must not become a permanent limitation on the student; it is a temporary bridge.
- **Vague Responses**: If student answers are vague and repetitive, the issue is weak conceptual understanding, not lack of effort.
